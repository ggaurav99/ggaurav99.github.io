01
Thesis · Epidemiology
SVITR Disease Model with Behavioural Dynamics

Built a coupled epidemiological-behavioural model where disease dynamics are shaped by individual decision-making. The key innovation: people don't perceive objective probabilities (like disease severity) at face value. Using the Prelec probability weighting function, we capture how optimism, skepticism, and information access distort perceived risk. Combined with hyperbolic discounting — which models how impatience affects decisions over time — the model computes perceived payoffs for each strategy (vaccination, antibiotics, or none). These payoffs then feed back into disease dynamics, making the model a genuine two-way system between behaviour and contagion.

02
Ongoing · Computational Neuroscience
Brain Controllability & Trait Anxiety Correlates

The standard way to diagnose trait anxiety is the STAI — a pen-and-paper self-report test that depends entirely on the honesty of the patient. We're building an objective alternative. Using Network Control Theory applied to brain connectomes, we assess the controllability of different brain regions — essentially, how easily each region can steer the brain into different activity states. The goal: find controllability metrics that correlate with STAI scores, so that a clinician can run brain scans and compute a diagnosis without the patient needing to self-report at all.

💭 On philosophy & expanding horizons

"Books allow me to inhabit minds shaped by completely different times, places, and circumstances — and that is the most reliable way I know to think beyond myself."

My relationship with philosophy began not in a classroom but through a personal inflection point — an experience that forced me to question whether what I believed was genuinely mine, or simply the product of my surroundings: family, peers, school, the particular corner of society I grew up in. It was, in retrospect, an encounter with survivorship bias — and I didn't survive it, metaphorically speaking.


That sent me to Sophie's World, then Will Durant, then a critical survey of Indian philosophy. What struck me was simple: people who lived centuries ago, in completely different parts of the world, with no shared cultural context — thought radically differently. If they could arrive at such different conclusions, how certain could I be about my own? Books became the tool for borrowing 60–70 years of someone else's thinking in 300 pages. That still strikes me as an extraordinary deal.


Today my interests span Stoicism, psychoanalytic theory (Freud, Lacan), the philosophy of mind, and the question of what it means to think freely in a world that is constantly trying to think for you. The mountains help too — there's something about altitude that strips away noise and forces clarity.
