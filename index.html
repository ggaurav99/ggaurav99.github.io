About me

I'm Gaurav, a recent graduate from Shiv Nadar University with a BSc (Research) in Mathematics.

My current research sits at the intersection of mathematical modelling and human behaviour — how people make decisions under uncertainty, and what happens when those decisions interact with large-scale dynamics like disease spread or brain activity.

I'm currently working on two projects: one coupling epidemiological dynamics with behavioural game theory and another applying network control theory to structural brain connectomes to find objective correlates of trait anxiety.

Outside of academics I like to read — philosophy, fiction, psychoanalytic theory and whenever I get time I go for a hike in the Himalayas !

Thesis · Epidemiology Almost Completed
SVITR Disease Model with Behavioural Dynamics

A coupled epidemiological-behavioural model where disease dynamics are shaped by individual decision-making and vice-versa . Using the Prelec probability weighting function, the model captures how optimism, scepticism, and information access distort perceived risk. Combined with hyperbolic discounting — which models how impatience affects decisions over time — the model computes perceived payoffs for each strategy (vaccination, antibiotics, or none). These payoffs feed back into disease dynamics, creating a genuine two-way system between behaviour and contagion.

Computational Neuroscience Ongoing
Brain Controllability & Trait Anxiety Correlates

The standard way to diagnose trait anxiety is the STAI — a self-report questionnaire dependent entirely on patient honesty. This research tries to find an objective alternative. Using Network Control Theory applied to brain connectomes, we assess the controllability of different brain regions: how easily each region can steer the brain into different activity states. The goal is to find controllability metrics that correlate with STAI scores, so a clinician can compute a diagnosis from brain scans without requiring self-report.

On philosophy & reading

"Books allow me to inhabit minds shaped by completely different times, places, and circumstances — and that is the most reliable way I know to think beyond myself."

My relationship with philosophy began not in a classroom but through a personal inflection point — an experience that forced me to question whether what I believed was genuinely mine, or simply the product of my surroundings: family, peers, school, the particular corner of society I grew up in. It was, in retrospect, an encounter with survivorship bias.


That sent me to Sophie's World, then Will Durant, then a critical survey of Indian philosophy. What struck me was simple: people who lived centuries ago, in completely different parts of the world, with no shared cultural context — thought radically differently. If they could arrive at such different conclusions, how certain could I be about my own? Books became the tool for borrowing 60–70 years of someone else's thinking in 300 pages. That still strikes me as an extraordinary deal.


Today my interests span Stoicism, psychoanalytic theory (Freud, Lacan), the philosophy of mind, and the question of what it means to think freely in a world that is constantly trying to think for you.
